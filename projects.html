State Education Bill

08/19 - Present; Assemblyman Evan Low

Through YAPA, I was quite happy to see the growth of my peers, and it excites me that I was able to create a passion for public service.

The week after YAPA, I started wondering if there was anything else I could do to expand my horizons regarding encouraging public service.
One thing that I was always aware of was that my high school (and after some research, pretty much all of California) educated only on federal governments and their engagement. YAPA prompted my questioning of this: if the majority of our tangible change occurs locally, why are we fixating on national politics?

Seeing the profound difference the YAPA curriculum had on some of our students, I now want everyone in California to receive some education of this form.
With Assemblyman Evan Low, I'm currently working to submit a bill to the California Assembly that will propose an addition to state curriculum to introduce local governments and their engagement to the students. If this passes, all schools will feature YAPA-esque education in their curricula, which is really exciting. Once I finalize the bill proposal and submission, I'll share it here. Maybe soon I'll have to campaign on behalf of my bill, but I'll focus on submitting it to the Assembly first.

Stanford Institutes of Medical Research (SIMR)

06/19 - 08/19

I spent a good chunk of my summer as part of the SIMR program, working under mentor Abhinav Kaushik as part of the Nadeau Lab. Our project sought to use topology as a solution to graph theory-based representation of gene networks. Instead of describing the technicalities of the project, I'll leave some pictures that go into more details.

I'm pretty happy with how this program turned out. I get along quite well with my mentor, and we are working to formalize the analysis and publish this work, which I'm very excited about. I'll definitely try to keep posting about the progress on that.

I would like to include the code that went behind all of these figures/analysis, but since we are redo-ing a large chunk of the analysis (mostly to make it more mathematically rigorous), I'd be much more content sharing the updated code when we finish the manuscript. For example, Pearson's coefficient for network recreation is not the most sophisticated technique and definitely won't be smiled upon by the reviewers. It simply is not rigorous enough, especially when the data is so huge and has many degrees of freedom. Needless to say, I'll have plenty to work on before this paper reaches the level I'd like it to be. Regardless, SIMR has given me a world of research opportunities, and I am excited for the future of this project!
